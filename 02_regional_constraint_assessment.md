## 1. Research Objective

Module 1 concluded that power availability is a credible investment constraint for AI infrastructure. Module 2 begins the next stage by identifying where that constraint is most likely to affect deployment.

Rather than determining which region is "best," this module seeks to identify where the available evidence suggests power availability may become a meaningful deployment constraint.

This module does not answer the broader capital allocation question. Instead, it focuses on regional constraint mapping and serves as an active research notebook.

The current scope is intentionally limited. Additional ISOs and RTOs may be incorporated if future evidence suggests they are material to the investment thesis.

## 3. Working Research Questions

1. Where are AI-related electricity demand pressures already visible in official forecasts or planning documents?
2. Which regions show the greatest mismatch between expected load growth and power delivery capacity?
3. Are current constraints primarily driven by generation availability, transmission capacity, interconnection timing, or forecasting uncertainty?
4. How do PJM, ERCOT, and CAISO differ in their treatment of large-load requests?
5. Which indicators should be standardized to enable consistent regional comparison?
6. Are data center loads treated as firm demand, speculative demand, or partially derated demand?
7. Which regions show evidence that large-load growth is changing transmission planning requirements?
8. How should AI-related data center demand be separated from other large-load categories such as crypto mining, hydrogen, manufacturing, and electrification?
9. Which indicators are likely to remain structurally relevant across multiple planning cycles rather than reflecting temporary market conditions?

## 4. Working Hypotheses

These are preliminary hypotheses and should not be interpreted as conclusions.

### Primary Working Hypothesis

PJM may experience more visible AI infrastructure deployment constraints than the other initial regions because data center demand is concentrated within specific load pockets and already appears to be influencing transmission planning.

### Secondary Working Hypothesis

ERCOT may exhibit faster headline load growth, but interpreting the constraint requires greater caution because large-load requests are adjusted for project delays, realization rates, and observed consumption patterns.

### Exploratory Working Hypothesis

CAISO may be less exposed to immediate AI data center deployment pressure than PJM or ERCOT, but its planning framework may provide useful insight into how large loads are incorporated into long-term resource and transmission planning.

### Cross-Region Working Hypothesis

Interconnection timing and transmission deliverability may become more informative indicators of deployment risk than aggregate generation capacity when comparing regional markets.

## 6. Interim Assessment

Current evidence suggests that PJM and ERCOT warrant the highest research priority, although additional validation is required before any regional ranking can be established.

PJM currently presents stronger evidence of concentrated data center demand and transmission relevance. The remaining question is whether the primary constraint reflects total capacity, deliverability into specific load pockets, or a combination of both.

ERCOT currently presents stronger evidence of rapid load growth and evolving planning methodology. The key research question is how much requested large-load demand should ultimately be treated as firm, flexible, delayed, or speculative.

CAISO remains valuable as a planning-process comparison. Current evidence does not yet establish whether it faces the same near-term AI infrastructure deployment pressure observed in PJM or ERCOT.

At this stage, the evidence supports continued regional comparison but remains insufficient to establish a definitive regional constraint ranking.

## 7. Remaining Questions

1. How much of PJM's forecasted large-load growth is concentrated within Northern Virginia compared with other zones?
2. Which PJM zones or LDAs exhibit the greatest mismatch between large-load additions and power deliverability?
3. Within ERCOT, what proportion of requested data center load should be treated as firm, flexible, delayed, or speculative?
4. Does ERCOT's curtailment framework improve deployment flexibility, or does it introduce a different form of power availability risk?
5. Within CAISO, are data center loads sufficiently concentrated to create local transmission or distribution constraints?
6. Which indicators should ultimately be standardized across regions—peak load growth, reserve margin, interconnection queue size, congestion, transmission violations, curtailment risk, or utility service application volume?
7. How should AI-related data center demand be separated from other categories of large-load growth?
8. What primary evidence is available at the utility territory level rather than only at the ISO or RTO level?

## 8. Next Research Steps

The next stage of this module is to build a standardized regional comparison framework across PJM, ERCOT, and CAISO. Candidate indicators include load growth, data center-specific demand (where available), reserve margins, transmission planning signals, interconnection activity, and large-load planning methodology.

Research will then move below the ISO/RTO level. For PJM, this includes Northern Virginia and other high-growth zones. For ERCOT, the focus shifts toward weather zones and transmission planning areas receiving significant large-load requests. For CAISO, the objective is to determine whether large-load applications are concentrated within specific utility territories.

Completion of this module will provide the foundation for **Module 3**, which examines who owns or controls the identified constraints—including utilities, transmission owners, generation owners, interconnection processes, regulators, and large-load customers.
